Car Maintenance Schedule: Complete Checklist by Mileage

Last updated: April 9, 2026

A neglected $40 air filter can drag 5% off your fuel economy. A skipped $80 transmission flush at 60,000 miles can set up a $3,500 rebuild five years later. That is the math that makes a maintenance schedule the highest-ROI habit any vehicle owner has. What follows is a mileage-indexed checklist — 3,000 miles, 15,000, 30,000, 60,000, 100,000, and beyond — built around the intervals the major manufacturers converge on, with notes where severe-service driving (short trips, heavy loads, dusty conditions, stop-and-go traffic) compresses them. Cross-reference your owner's manual where it differs, especially for modern vehicles with oil-life monitors, but use this page as your default when you want to know what is due next and how much of it you can do in your own driveway. Pair it with DIY oil changes and brake-pad replacement to start cutting shop bills immediately.

Owner's Manual First

This guide provides general maintenance intervals for most passenger vehicles. Your owner's manual contains the manufacturer's specific recommendations for your vehicle. Always defer to your manual when intervals differ, especially for newer vehicles with extended service intervals based on oil life monitoring systems.

Every 3,000 to 5,000 Miles: Routine Checks

Oil and Filter Change

  • Conventional oil: Change every 3,000 to 5,000 miles
  • Synthetic oil: Change every 7,500 to 10,000 miles (some manufacturers allow up to 15,000)
  • Always replace the oil filter at every oil change
  • Check oil level between changes and top off if needed

Oil is the lifeblood of your engine. It lubricates moving parts, dissipates heat, and carries away contaminants. Old, degraded oil loses its protective properties and can lead to accelerated engine wear, sludge buildup, and eventually engine failure. Learn how to do this yourself with our complete oil change guide.

Tire Rotation and Pressure Check

  • Rotate tires every 5,000 to 7,500 miles
  • Check tire pressure monthly and before long trips (use the placard on the driver's door jamb, not the tire sidewall)
  • Inspect tread depth with the penny test: insert a penny upside down into the tread; if you can see all of Lincoln's head, the tire needs replacing
  • Look for uneven wear patterns that indicate alignment issues

Fluid Level Checks

  • Engine oil level
  • Coolant level (check when engine is cold)
  • Brake fluid level
  • Power steering fluid (if equipped)
  • Windshield washer fluid
  • Transmission fluid (if equipped with a dipstick)

Every 15,000 Miles: Inspection Interval

Brake System Inspection

  • Measure brake pad thickness (replace at 3mm or less)
  • Inspect rotors for scoring, warping, or minimum thickness
  • Check brake lines and hoses for cracks, leaks, or swelling
  • Test parking brake function

Brakes are your vehicle's most important safety system. Regular inspection catches wear before it becomes dangerous. If your pads are getting thin, check out our guide to changing brake pads.

Engine Air Filter

  • Replace every 15,000 to 30,000 miles depending on driving conditions
  • Replace more frequently if you drive on dusty or unpaved roads
  • A clogged air filter reduces engine performance and fuel economy

Cabin Air Filter

  • Replace every 15,000 to 25,000 miles
  • A dirty cabin filter reduces HVAC airflow and can cause musty odors
  • This is one of the easiest DIY replacements, usually requiring no tools

Every 30,000 Miles: Major Service Interval

Transmission Fluid Service

  • Automatic transmission: Fluid change or flush every 30,000 to 60,000 miles
  • Manual transmission: Gear oil change every 30,000 to 60,000 miles
  • Use only the manufacturer-specified fluid type (ATF type matters)
  • Dark or burnt-smelling transmission fluid indicates overdue service

Coolant System Service

  • Flush and replace coolant every 30,000 miles for conventional coolant
  • Extended-life coolant can last up to 100,000 miles (check your manual)
  • Inspect hoses for soft spots, cracks, or swelling
  • Test coolant concentration with a refractometer or test strip
  • Check the radiator cap seal and pressure rating

Spark Plugs

  • Copper plugs: Replace every 30,000 miles
  • Platinum plugs: Replace every 60,000 miles
  • Iridium plugs: Replace every 60,000 to 100,000 miles
  • Worn spark plugs cause misfires, poor fuel economy, and rough idle
  • Always gap new plugs to manufacturer specification before installation

Brake Fluid Flush

  • Replace every 30,000 miles or every 2 years
  • Brake fluid is hygroscopic, meaning it absorbs moisture over time
  • Moisture-contaminated fluid lowers the boiling point and can cause brake fade under heavy use
  • Use only the DOT rating specified in your owner's manual

Every 60,000 Miles: Extended Service

Serpentine Belt

  • Inspect for cracks, fraying, glazing, or chunk loss every 30,000 miles
  • Replace every 60,000 to 100,000 miles even if it looks okay
  • A broken serpentine belt disables the alternator, power steering, water pump, and AC simultaneously
  • Replace the belt tensioner at the same time if it shows wear

Battery

  • Have the battery load-tested annually after 3 years
  • Most batteries last 4 to 6 years depending on climate
  • Clean terminal corrosion with a baking soda and water solution
  • Ensure the battery is properly secured to prevent vibration damage
  • Extreme heat actually degrades batteries faster than cold

Timing Belt (If Equipped)

  • Replace every 60,000 to 100,000 miles (check your specific vehicle)
  • Many modern vehicles use timing chains that do not require scheduled replacement
  • If your engine is an interference design, a broken timing belt causes catastrophic engine damage
  • Replace the water pump at the same time since it is behind the timing cover
  • This is a complex job that many DIYers leave to a professional

Every 100,000 Miles: High-Mileage Milestones

Complete Suspension Inspection

  • Inspect shocks and struts for leaks and bounce test
  • Check ball joints, tie rod ends, and control arm bushings for play
  • Inspect wheel bearings for noise or looseness
  • Replace worn sway bar links and end links
  • Get a full alignment after any suspension component replacement

Complete Fluid Renewal

  • Flush all fluids: oil, coolant, transmission, brake, and power steering
  • Replace differential fluid (rear-wheel and all-wheel drive vehicles)
  • Replace transfer case fluid (4WD and AWD vehicles)

Additional High-Mileage Checks

  • Inspect exhaust system for rust, leaks, and hanger condition
  • Check CV joint boots and axles for cracks and grease leakage
  • Consider switching to high-mileage oil formulation
  • Inspect fuel system including fuel filter replacement
  • Have the catalytic converter tested for efficiency

Severe Driving Conditions

If any of the following apply to your driving habits, follow the manufacturer's "severe" or "heavy-duty" maintenance schedule, which typically cuts service intervals in half:

  • Short trips: Driving less than 10 miles per trip, especially in cold weather
  • Stop-and-go traffic: Frequent city driving or commuting in heavy traffic
  • Dusty or gravel roads: Driving on unpaved surfaces regularly
  • Towing or hauling: Regularly pulling a trailer or carrying heavy loads
  • Extreme temperatures: Very hot or very cold climates
  • Mountainous driving: Frequent steep grades that stress the engine, brakes, and transmission

Many drivers fall into the severe category without realizing it. If your daily commute involves short trips in city traffic, you are likely in the severe schedule category. Setting up a dedicated garage workshop makes it much easier to stay on top of these more frequent service intervals.

Frequently Asked Questions

Most modern vehicles with synthetic oil can go 7,500 to 10,000 miles between oil changes, while conventional oil typically requires changes every 3,000 to 5,000 miles. Always follow your vehicle manufacturer's recommended interval found in your owner's manual, as this can vary significantly based on engine design, oil type, and driving conditions.

Skipping scheduled maintenance can lead to accelerated wear, reduced fuel efficiency, unexpected breakdowns, and costly repairs. For example, neglecting oil changes can cause sludge buildup and engine damage, while ignoring timing belt replacement can result in catastrophic engine failure. Deferred maintenance almost always costs more in the long run than preventive care.

Follow the owner's manual intervals unless you drive in severe conditions such as frequent short trips, dusty environments, towing, or extreme temperatures, in which case the severe duty schedule applies.

Tires should be rotated every 5,000 to 7,500 miles, or at every other oil change. Regular rotation ensures even wear across all four tires, extends tire life, and maintains balanced handling. If you notice uneven wear patterns, have your alignment checked as well, since misalignment can cause rapid tire wear regardless of rotation frequency.

Oil changes and brake inspections are the two most critical maintenance items. Clean oil protects your engine from wear and overheating, while functional brakes are essential for safety. After those, tire maintenance (including rotation, pressure checks, and alignment), coolant system service, and transmission fluid changes are the next most important items for vehicle longevity and reliability.
