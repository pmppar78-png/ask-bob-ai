Bathroom Remodel Guide: Planning, Costs, and Step-by-Step Tips

A bathroom remodel is one of the most popular home improvement projects, and for good reason. A well-planned bathroom renovation can dramatically improve your daily routine, increase your home's value, and address lingering issues like poor ventilation, outdated plumbing, or wasted space. Whether you are refreshing a powder room or gutting a master bath, this guide walks you through every stage of the process.

Setting Your Bathroom Remodel Budget

Before selecting tile samples or browsing fixtures, you need a realistic budget. Bathroom remodels have a wide cost range depending on the scope of work, material quality, and whether you hire professionals or do some work yourself. The most common mistake homeowners make is underestimating the budget, so plan for contingencies from the start.

Basic Refresh

$3,000 - $7,000

New paint, fixtures, hardware, mirror, and accessories. Keeps existing layout and plumbing in place.

Mid-Range Remodel

$10,000 - $25,000

New tile, vanity, toilet, tub or shower, lighting, and flooring. Same layout, updated everything.

High-End / Gut Remodel

$25,000 - $50,000+

Custom tile, premium fixtures, layout changes, moved plumbing, heated floors, custom cabinetry.

Budget Rule of Thumb

A common guideline is to spend no more than 5-10% of your home's value on a bathroom remodel. Always set aside 15-20% of your budget as a contingency fund for unexpected issues like hidden water damage, mold, or outdated plumbing that needs replacement once walls are opened.

Planning Your Bathroom Layout

The layout is the foundation of your remodel. Changing the position of the toilet, shower, or vanity requires moving plumbing supply and drain lines, which significantly increases cost and complexity. Before deciding on a new layout, understand what is involved.

Keeping the Existing Layout

The most cost-effective approach is to keep fixtures in their current positions. This avoids the expense of rerouting plumbing, which can involve opening floors and walls, modifying drain lines, and potentially requiring engineering assessment for load-bearing walls. Even with the same layout, you can completely transform the look and feel of a bathroom with new fixtures, tile, and finishes.

Modifying the Layout

If your current layout is genuinely dysfunctional, a layout change may be worth the investment. Common layout changes include swapping a tub for a walk-in shower, adding a double vanity, moving the toilet for better clearance, or expanding into an adjacent closet. Each of these changes requires careful planning around plumbing rough-in locations, drain slopes, vent stacks, and building codes.

Most jurisdictions require minimum clearances: 15 inches from the center of the toilet to any side wall or obstruction, 21 inches of clear space in front of the toilet, and a minimum shower size of 30 by 30 inches (though 36 by 36 is far more comfortable). Check your local codes, as some jurisdictions have stricter requirements.

Choosing Fixtures and Materials

Toilets

Modern toilets use 1.28 gallons per flush or less, compared to 3.5 to 7 gallons for older models. When replacing a toilet, measure the rough-in distance (the distance from the wall to the center of the drain flange), which is typically 12 inches but can be 10 or 14 inches in older homes. Key decisions include one-piece vs. two-piece (one-piece is easier to clean but heavier to install), round vs. elongated bowl (elongated is more comfortable but requires more space), and standard vs. comfort height (comfort height is about 17 to 19 inches and is easier for most adults).

Vanity and Sink

The vanity is often the focal point of a bathroom. Stock vanities from home improvement stores are the most affordable option, while semi-custom and custom vanities offer more sizes, finishes, and configurations. Consider the countertop material carefully: quartz is durable and low-maintenance, granite adds natural beauty, solid surface is budget-friendly, and marble is luxurious but requires sealing and is prone to etching from acidic products.

Shower and Tub Options

Your shower or tub choice has the biggest impact on both the look and the budget. Options include acrylic tub-shower surrounds (most affordable, $300-$800), tile shower with a prefab pan ($1,500-$4,000 installed), custom tile shower with a mortar bed or linear drain ($3,000-$8,000+ installed), freestanding soaking tub ($500-$3,000 for the tub alone), and frameless glass shower enclosures ($1,000-$3,000 for the glass). If you are choosing tile, hire an experienced tile installer or invest significant time learning proper waterproofing techniques before attempting it yourself, as improper waterproofing is the number one cause of bathroom water damage.

Tiling Your Bathroom

Tile selection and installation are critical decisions. For floor tile, choose a material with adequate slip resistance, rated at least 0.42 on the DCOF (Dynamic Coefficient of Friction) scale for wet areas. Porcelain tile is the most popular choice for bathrooms because of its low water absorption rate, durability, and wide range of styles.

For shower walls, proper waterproofing behind the tile is essential. Modern best practices use either a sheet membrane (like Kerdi) or a liquid-applied membrane (like RedGard) over cement board. Never install tile over standard drywall or green board in a shower; it will eventually fail and cause water damage.

Tile Installation Tips

  • Dry-lay your tile pattern before applying any mortar to check the layout and minimize narrow cuts at edges.
  • Use the correct thinset for your tile. Large-format tiles (12 inches and larger) require a large-and-heavy-tile mortar.
  • Use tile spacers consistently for even grout lines. Popular grout line widths are 1/16 inch for a modern look and 1/8 inch for a traditional look.
  • Seal grout lines after curing (typically 48-72 hours) to prevent staining and moisture absorption.
  • Use a quality wet saw for clean, precise cuts. Rented tile saws from home improvement stores work well for most projects.

Flooring Considerations

Bathroom flooring must handle moisture, frequent cleaning, and foot traffic. The best options include porcelain or ceramic tile (the gold standard for bathroom floors), luxury vinyl plank or tile (waterproof, comfortable underfoot, and increasingly realistic), and natural stone (beautiful but requires sealing and can be slippery when wet). Avoid laminate flooring and hardwood in bathrooms, as both are susceptible to water damage despite marketing claims to the contrary.

Lighting and Ventilation

Good lighting transforms a bathroom. Plan for three types of lighting: ambient lighting (overhead fixture or recessed lights for general illumination), task lighting (sconces flanking the mirror at face level, not overhead, to eliminate shadows), and accent lighting (optional LED strips under a floating vanity or in a shower niche for visual interest).

Adequate ventilation is not optional. A bathroom exhaust fan rated for your room size (1 CFM per square foot, minimum 50 CFM) prevents moisture buildup that causes mold, peeling paint, and structural damage. Run the fan during showers and for at least 20 minutes afterward. Consider a fan with a humidity sensor that turns on automatically when moisture is detected.

Plumbing Considerations

Plumbing work is where bathroom remodels most often require professional help. While replacing a faucet or toilet is within reach for many homeowners, moving supply lines, modifying drain pipes, or working with the vent stack should be handled by a licensed plumber. Key plumbing considerations include:

  • Water shut-off valves: Ensure each fixture has its own shut-off valve. Replace old gate valves with modern quarter-turn ball valves while walls are open.
  • Supply line sizing: Undersized supply lines cause low water pressure. If your home has older galvanized pipes, consider upgrading to copper or PEX during the remodel.
  • Drain slope: Drain pipes must slope at 1/4 inch per foot toward the main drain. Moving a fixture too far from the existing drain can create slope problems.
  • Venting: Every drain fixture requires proper venting to prevent siphoning of the trap seal. Moving fixtures may require adding or modifying vent pipes.

Permits and Building Codes

Most bathroom remodels that involve plumbing modifications, electrical changes, or structural work require building permits. The permit process ensures work is done safely and to code. Typical requirements include GFCI protection on all bathroom outlets, a dedicated 20-amp circuit for bathroom receptacles, proper ventilation (a window or exhaust fan), waterproofing in wet areas, and minimum fixture clearances.

Do not skip permits to save money or time. Unpermitted work can create significant problems when selling your home, as inspectors and buyers will identify non-code-compliant work. Insurance companies may also deny claims related to unpermitted modifications.

Bathroom Remodel Timeline

Understanding the typical sequence of work helps you plan around being without a bathroom. A standard mid-range remodel follows this general order:

  1. Demolition (1-2 days): Remove old fixtures, tile, and drywall as needed.
  2. Rough plumbing and electrical (2-3 days): Move or add supply lines, drains, and electrical circuits.
  3. Inspection: Schedule required rough-in inspections before closing walls.
  4. Cement board and waterproofing (1-2 days): Install substrate and waterproof membrane in wet areas.
  5. Tile installation (3-5 days): Install floor and wall tile, including cure time for mortar and grout.
  6. Vanity and toilet installation (1 day): Set the vanity, mount the sink, and install the toilet.
  7. Fixtures and trim (1-2 days): Install faucets, showerhead, lighting, mirror, and accessories.
  8. Paint and final touches (1 day): Paint walls and ceiling, caulk, and clean up.
  9. Final inspection: Schedule required final inspections.

DIY vs. Hiring a Professional

A bathroom remodel often involves a mix of DIY and professional work. Here is a realistic breakdown of what most homeowners can handle versus what typically requires a pro.

Good DIY tasks: Demolition (with proper safety precautions), painting, installing a toilet, mounting a vanity mirror, installing towel bars and accessories, replacing cabinet hardware, and simple faucet replacement.

Hire a professional for: Moving or modifying plumbing lines, electrical circuit additions or modifications, tile work in wet areas (unless you have experience), shower waterproofing, structural modifications, and anything requiring a permit and inspection.

Frequently Asked Questions

Bathroom remodel costs vary widely based on scope, materials, and location. A basic refresh with new paint, fixtures, and hardware typically runs $3,000 to $7,000. A mid-range remodel with new tile, vanity, toilet, and tub/shower surround usually costs $10,000 to $25,000. A high-end or full gut remodel with custom tile, premium fixtures, and layout changes can range from $25,000 to $50,000 or more. Labor typically accounts for 40-60% of the total budget.

Permit requirements depend on the scope of work and your local jurisdiction. Cosmetic updates like painting, replacing fixtures in the same location, and installing new hardware generally do not require permits. However, moving plumbing, adding or modifying electrical circuits, changing the layout, or making structural modifications almost always require permits and inspections. Always check with your local building department before starting work. Unpermitted work can create problems when selling your home and may not be covered by insurance.

A cosmetic refresh typically takes 1 to 2 weeks. A mid-range remodel where you are replacing the tub, vanity, toilet, and tile usually takes 3 to 5 weeks. A full gut remodel involving layout changes and moving plumbing can take 6 to 10 weeks or longer, depending on permit timelines, material lead times, and contractor availability. Always add a 20-30% time buffer to your estimate for unexpected issues like hidden water damage or plumbing problems discovered during demolition.

Several bathroom remodel tasks are well-suited for DIY. Painting walls and ceilings, installing new cabinet hardware, replacing a toilet (if the new one uses the same rough-in distance), installing a vanity mirror, updating light fixtures, installing towel bars and accessories, and painting or refinishing a vanity are all manageable for most homeowners. Tiling is a popular DIY task but has a steep learning curve. Plumbing modifications, electrical work, and anything structural should typically be handled by licensed professionals.

Walk-in showers are more accessible, easier to clean, and can make a small bathroom feel larger. For resale value, real estate experts generally recommend having at least one bathtub in the home, ideally in the main bathroom. A popular compromise is a tub-shower combination in the main bath and a walk-in shower in the master bath.
