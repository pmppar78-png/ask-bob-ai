Kitchen Renovation Planning Guide: Budget, Timeline, and Steps

By Paul Paradis · Last updated: April 14, 2026

The 2025 Remodeling Impact Report puts a mid-range kitchen renovation at a 72% cost recovery at resale — but only if the layout reads as intentional, not improvised. Budget realistically: a cosmetic refresh (paint, hardware, countertop-only, same layout) lands $8,000-$18,000. A mid-range pull-and-replace with new cabinetry in the existing footprint runs $25,000-$55,000. An upscale renovation that moves walls, relocates the sink, and upgrades to semi-custom cabinets and stone counters pushes $75,000-$150,000. The difference between a finished job and a year of delays is almost always cabinet lead times (8-16 weeks for semi-custom, 2-4 weeks for stock) — order those before demo, not during. Layout drives everything else: a working kitchen triangle (sink-range-fridge legs 4-9 feet each, total 13-26 feet) is still the single best predictor of whether the room will feel right once it's done. The guide below walks the order a GC actually runs the job — design, ordering, demo, rough-in, drywall/paint, flooring, cabinets, counters, tile backsplash, appliances, punch list — and flags the decisions where homeowners most often blow the budget. The Golden Rule of Kitchen Renovation

Plan thoroughly before you demolish anything. The planning phase should take longer than the construction phase. Rushing into demolition before finalizing your design, ordering materials, and scheduling trades is the number one cause of budget overruns, timeline delays, and homeowner regret. Take the time to get it right on paper before you pick up a sledgehammer.

Setting Your Kitchen Renovation Budget

Your budget determines the scope of your renovation. Be honest about what you can afford, and always include a contingency fund for surprises behind the walls. A common guideline is to spend 5-15% of your home's value on a kitchen renovation, but the right budget is the one that makes financial sense for your situation.

The contingency fund is not optional. Once walls are opened, you may discover outdated wiring that raises electrical safety concerns, water damage, inadequate framing, or plumbing that does not meet current code. These issues must be addressed, and they cost money. Experienced contractors will tell you that surprises appear in nearly every kitchen renovation.

Choosing Your Kitchen Layout

The layout of your kitchen determines how efficiently you can cook, clean, and move through the space. The classic "work triangle" concept places your sink, stove, and refrigerator at three points of a triangle, minimizing steps between the most-used stations. While modern kitchen design has evolved beyond strict adherence to the triangle, the principle of efficient workflow remains important.

Galley Kitchen

Best for: Narrow spaces, apartments, smaller homes

Two parallel runs of cabinets and counters with a walkway between them. Galley kitchens are extremely efficient for cooking because everything is within arm's reach. The recommended aisle width is 42-48 inches for a single cook, 48-60 inches if two people cook together. The main limitation is that galley layouts do not accommodate seating or socializing within the kitchen.

L-Shaped Kitchen

Best for: Open floor plans, medium-sized kitchens

Cabinets and counters along two perpendicular walls, forming an L shape. This layout opens one or two sides of the kitchen to adjacent rooms, making it ideal for open-concept living. It provides good counter space and a natural work triangle. The open side can accommodate a dining table or island for additional workspace and seating.

U-Shaped Kitchen

Best for: Larger rooms, serious cooks who want maximum storage

Cabinets and counters on three walls, creating a U shape. This layout maximizes storage and counter space, providing the most usable workspace of any standard layout. It works best in rooms at least 10 feet wide to avoid feeling cramped. The enclosed design can feel isolated from living areas, so consider a pass-through window or partial wall to maintain connection.

Kitchen Island

Best for: Large kitchens, entertaining, multi-cook households

An island adds a freestanding work surface in the center of the kitchen. It can house a sink, cooktop, dishwasher, or simply provide extra counter space and seating. An island requires at least 42 inches of clearance on all sides (48 inches is better) and works best in kitchens that are at least 12 feet wide. Islands with plumbing or gas require significant under-floor work that adds to cost and complexity.

Selecting Cabinets

Cabinets are the largest single expense in most kitchen renovations and have the biggest visual impact. Understanding the three tiers of cabinetry helps you allocate your budget wisely.

Stock Cabinets

Pre-manufactured in standard sizes and finishes, stock cabinets are the most affordable option at $60-$200 per linear foot installed. They are available immediately or within a few days. The trade-off is limited size options, fewer finish choices, and basic construction quality. Stock cabinets work well for standard kitchen layouts and budget-conscious renovations.

Semi-Custom Cabinets

Built to order in a factory using standard construction methods but with more size options, finish choices, and interior accessories. Semi-custom cabinets cost $150-$400 per linear foot installed with a lead time of 4-8 weeks. They offer the best balance of customization, quality, and cost for most homeowners.

Custom Cabinets

Built by a cabinetmaker to your exact specifications with unlimited size, finish, material, and design options. Custom cabinets cost $400-$1,000+ per linear foot installed with a lead time of 8-16 weeks. They are the best choice for unusual room dimensions, specific design visions, or homeowners who prioritize quality and craftsmanship above budget constraints.

Countertop Options

Your countertop choice affects the look, function, and daily maintenance of your kitchen. Here are the most popular options:

  • Granite: Natural stone with unique veining, extremely durable, heat-resistant. $50-$200 per square foot installed. Requires periodic sealing.
  • Quartz (engineered stone): Non-porous, uniform appearance, zero maintenance. $50-$150 per square foot installed. The most popular choice for modern kitchens.
  • Butcher block: Warm, natural wood surface that is food-safe and repairable. $40-$100 per square foot installed. Requires regular oiling and is susceptible to water damage near sinks.
  • Laminate: Affordable and available in hundreds of patterns. $10-$40 per square foot installed. Modern laminates are far more attractive and durable than older versions.
  • Marble: Luxurious appearance, cool surface ideal for baking. $75-$250 per square foot installed. Porous and stains easily; requires diligent maintenance.
  • Concrete: Industrial aesthetic, fully customizable shapes and colors. $65-$135 per square foot installed. Requires sealing and can develop hairline cracks over time.

Flooring for Kitchens

Kitchen floors must withstand heavy foot traffic, dropped items, water spills, and frequent cleaning. The best kitchen flooring options balance durability, comfort, and appearance:

  • Luxury vinyl plank (LVP): Waterproof, comfortable underfoot, affordable ($3-$7 per sq ft), and available in realistic wood and stone patterns. The most practical choice for most kitchen renovations.
  • Ceramic or porcelain tile: Extremely durable and waterproof ($5-$15 per sq ft installed). Hard underfoot for extended standing; use anti-fatigue mats at the sink and stove.
  • Hardwood: Beautiful and warm ($8-$15 per sq ft installed), but susceptible to water damage and scratching. Site-finished hardwood with multiple coats of polyurethane provides the best water resistance.
  • Natural stone: Premium appearance ($10-$30 per sq ft installed), extremely durable, but cold underfoot and requires sealing. Consider radiant floor heating for comfort.

Appliance Planning

Select appliances early in the design process because their dimensions determine cabinet and counter layouts. Measure the exact specifications of each appliance and confirm clearances for doors, drawers, and ventilation. Make sure you have the essential tools needed for accurate measuring and installation. Key considerations:

  • Refrigerator: Counter-depth models create a sleek, built-in look. Standard-depth models hold more but protrude past the counters. Measure the door swing to ensure it clears adjacent cabinets and walls.
  • Range or cooktop/wall oven: Gas requires a gas line (or propane conversion). Induction cooktops are gaining popularity for their speed, safety, and energy efficiency. Slide-in ranges provide a cleaner look than freestanding models.
  • Dishwasher: Standard width is 24 inches. Place it adjacent to the sink for efficient plumbing connections. Panel-ready models accept a custom front panel to match your cabinetry.
  • Ventilation: A proper range hood vented to the exterior is far more effective than a recirculating microwave hood. Plan the ductwork route early; it may need to pass through cabinets or the ceiling.

Lighting Design

Good kitchen lighting uses three layers to create a functional and inviting space:

  • Ambient lighting: Overall room illumination from recessed ceiling lights or a central fixture. Plan one recessed light per 20-25 square feet of floor space.
  • Task lighting: Under-cabinet LED strips or puck lights that illuminate countertop work surfaces. These eliminate shadows cast by upper cabinets and make food preparation safer and easier.
  • Accent lighting: In-cabinet lights, above-cabinet lighting, or pendant lights over an island that add visual interest and warmth. These are the finishing touch that makes a kitchen feel designed rather than just functional.

Plan all lighting locations before cabinets are installed, as electrical rough-in needs to happen during the framing stage. Consider dimmer switches for ambient and accent lighting to control mood and energy use.

Permits and Building Codes

Kitchen renovations frequently require permits, especially when they involve:

  • Electrical work (new circuits, outlet additions, panel upgrades)
  • Plumbing changes (moving sinks, adding dishwasher lines, gas connections)
  • Structural modifications (removing or modifying load-bearing walls)
  • Ventilation ductwork changes

Permit costs typically range from $200 to $1,500 depending on scope and jurisdiction. The permit process includes plan review, inspections at key stages, and a final sign-off. Skipping permits creates problems when selling your home, may void insurance coverage, and can result in unsafe installations. Deciding whether to DIY or hire a professional often comes down to whether the work requires permits and licensed tradespeople.

Renovation Timeline

A well-planned mid-range kitchen renovation follows this general timeline:

  1. Weeks 1-4: Planning and design. Finalize layout, select materials, get contractor bids, and apply for permits.
  2. Weeks 4-10: Material ordering. Order cabinets (longest lead time), countertops, appliances, flooring, and fixtures. Use this time to set up a temporary kitchen.
  3. Week 10-11: Demolition. Remove old cabinets, countertops, flooring, and appliances. This reveals the condition of walls, floors, plumbing, and wiring.
  4. Week 11-12: Rough-in work. Electrical, plumbing, and any structural modifications. Inspections happen at this stage.
  5. Week 12-13: Drywall and patching. Repair walls, add any new drywall, tape, mud, and sand smooth.
  6. Week 13-14: Cabinet installation. Upper and lower cabinets are leveled, shimmed, and secured to walls.
  7. Week 14-15: Countertop templating and installation. Templates are made after cabinets are installed; fabrication takes 1-2 weeks.
  8. Week 15-16: Flooring installation. Timing depends on material; some flooring goes in before cabinets, some after.
  9. Week 16-17: Finishing work. Backsplash tile, appliance installation, plumbing connections, light fixtures, hardware, trim, and painting.
  10. Week 17-18: Punch list and final inspection. Address any remaining details and schedule final building inspection.

Living Through a Kitchen Renovation

Surviving without a kitchen for 6-12 weeks requires preparation:

  • Set up a temporary kitchen in a dining room, basement, or garage with a folding table, microwave, mini-fridge, electric kettle, and portable induction cooktop
  • Stock up on paper plates, disposable utensils, and easy-prep meals
  • Plan a realistic food budget that includes takeout and restaurant meals
  • Seal off the kitchen doorway with plastic sheeting to contain dust (construction dust gets everywhere)
  • Establish a dish-washing station using a utility sink or large plastic tub
  • Keep a sense of humor; the temporary inconvenience leads to a permanent upgrade

Common Kitchen Renovation Mistakes

Learn from the mistakes of others to keep your renovation on track:

  • Insufficient storage: Prioritizing aesthetics over storage leads to cluttered countertops. Plan storage for everything you use daily.
  • Too few outlets: Modern kitchens need outlets every 4 feet along countertops (code requirement) plus dedicated circuits for major appliances. Plan for small appliance use.
  • Poor lighting: Relying on a single overhead fixture creates shadows everywhere. Layer your lighting as described above.
  • Ignoring the work triangle: Placing the sink, stove, and refrigerator too far apart or in a line makes cooking inefficient and tiring.
  • Choosing style over function: That open shelving looks great in magazines, but consider how it looks with your actual dishes, spices, and daily clutter.
  • Skipping the contingency fund: Hidden problems are almost guaranteed in older homes. Budget 10-15% above your planned spend.

Frequently Asked Questions About Kitchen Renovation

Kitchen renovation costs vary widely based on scope and materials. A minor refresh (paint, hardware, countertops) costs $5,000 to $15,000. A mid-range remodel with new cabinets, countertops, flooring, and appliances costs $25,000 to $60,000. A high-end renovation with custom cabinetry, premium surfaces, and structural changes can cost $75,000 to $150,000 or more. Budget 10-15% of your home's value as a guideline for renovation spend.

A minor kitchen refresh takes 2-4 weeks. A mid-range remodel typically takes 6-10 weeks from demolition to completion. Major renovations involving structural changes, permit approvals, and custom work can take 12-20 weeks or longer. Material lead times, especially for custom cabinets (6-12 weeks) and specialty countertops (2-4 weeks), are often the biggest factor in timeline length. Order materials well before your planned start date.

Yes, most homeowners stay in their homes during kitchen renovations, though it requires planning. Set up a temporary kitchen in another room with a microwave, mini-fridge, electric kettle, and portable cooktop. Expect dust, noise, and limited water access during certain phases. Plan for more takeout meals than usual. For major renovations lasting 3+ months with significant structural or plumbing work, some families choose temporary housing for comfort.

The best layout depends on your room dimensions and how you use the kitchen. Galley kitchens maximize efficiency in narrow spaces. L-shaped layouts work well in open floor plans and provide good counter space. U-shaped kitchens offer maximum storage and counter area for larger rooms. Islands add workspace and seating but require at least 42 inches of clearance on all sides. The work triangle concept (sink, stove, refrigerator forming a triangle) should guide placement regardless of layout.

For simple updates like painting, replacing hardware, and swapping faucets, DIY is practical for handy homeowners. For anything involving plumbing rerouting, electrical work, wall removal, or gas line modifications, hiring licensed professionals is essential for safety and code compliance. A general contractor adds 15-25% to project cost but manages scheduling, subcontractors, permits, and inspections. This is worth it for medium to large renovations where coordinating multiple trades is complex.
